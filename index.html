‚öñÔ∏è Ohio Compliance & Transport Protocol (ORC 3780.35)

LEGAL WARNING: This detailed breakdown is for educational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. Laws are dynamic. Your grow must strictly adhere to the Ohio Revised Code ($\text{ORC}$) concerning personal cultivation, possession, and transport. Always consult the most current $\text{ORC}$ text and a legal professional.

Deep Dive: Cultivation Requirements

  • **Primary Residence Mandate:** Growing is strictly limited to the adult's ($\text{21+}$) **primary residence**. Any grow site outside of this is a felony offense.
  • **Plant Count Enforcement:** **Household Maximum ($\text{12 plants}$):** This is non-negotiable. Exceeding this limit is a felony. Plants must be securely enclosed (locked room, locked tent, locked cabinet).
  • **Visibility Check (The "Unaided Vision" Rule):** Plants must **not** be visible "using normal unaided vision" from any public space. Compliance requires opaque, secure fencing ($\text{6-8}$ feet high) for outdoor sites, or non-visible indoor placement.
  • **Plant Definition:** While ambiguity exists, treat **any rooted plant** (beyond the unrooted cutting stage) as counting toward the $\text{12-plant}$ limit.

Possession, Storage, and Transport Limits

Possession within the Primary Residence

The $\text{ORC}$ allows an individual $\text{21+}$ to possess up to $\text{2.5}$ ounces of flower outside the home. However, the amount cultivated, processed, and stored **at the primary residence** derived from personal cultivation is not subject to this $\text{2.5 oz}$ limit, provided it is **securely locked** and solely for personal use. Storage must prevent access by minors ($\text{under 21}$).

Transport Protocol

  • **Transport Limit:** You may only transport up to **$\text{2.5}$ ounces of flower** or **$\text{15}$ grams of concentrate** at any given time.
  • **Secure Transport:** Cannabis must be transported in a **closed, sealed container** and should not be readily accessible to the driver (e.g., stored in the trunk or a locked glove compartment).
  • **Public Consumption:** **STRICTLY PROHIBITED.** Consumption is illegal in all public places, vehicles, and most multi-unit residential common areas.

üí° Indoor CEA: High-Frequency Fertigation (HFF)

Chapter 1: Advanced Substrate Management

Coco Coir: Buffering and Run-to-Waste (HFF)

Coco coir is the industry-standard choice for $\text{HFF}$. It offers rapid oxygen exchange and excellent water retention but demands precision:

  • **Mandatory Buffering:** Always pre-buffer coco with a **Calcium/Magnesium solution** before use. Unbuffered coco will bind $\text{Ca}/\text{Mg}$ from your nutrient solution, guaranteeing deficiencies.
  • **HFF Protocol:** Feed the plant **$\text{3-6}$ times daily** (depending on light intensity and plant size) using a complete nutrient solution. Each feed should produce $\text{15-30}\%$ runoff. This flushes old, stale water and salts, replenishing the root zone with fresh $\text{EC}$ and oxygen.
  • **Root Zone EC ($\text{RZEC}$):** Monitoring $\text{RZEC}$ via runoff is critical. Aim for runoff $\text{EC}$ to be no more than $\text{0.2-0.4}$ $\text{mS}/\text{cm}$ higher than your input $\text{EC}$ to confirm effective flushing and proper uptake.

Chapter 2: Light Physics and DLI Optimization

Photoperiodicity and End-of-Day (EOD) Treatments

Light intensity ($\text{PPFD}$) and duration ($\text{DLI}$) are the ultimate yield drivers. Use the **Calculators** tab to set your targets.

  • **Flowering Trigger:** The switch from $\text{18/6}$ to $\text{12/12}$ initiates flowering. Light leaks during the $\text{12-hour}$ dark period are a major stressor and can trigger **hermaphroditism**. Ensure your space is $100\%$ light-proof.
  • **Far-Red ($\text{FR}$) EOD Treatment:** By providing $\text{Far-Red}$ light ($\text{700-740nm}$) for $\text{10-15}$ minutes immediately after the main lights turn off, you can accelerate the sleep phase. This shortens the time it takes the plant to initiate flowering (up to $\text{5-7}$ days faster) by resetting the phytochrome clock.
  • **High-Intensity CO2 Boost:** When $\text{PPFD}$ levels exceed $1000$ $\mu\text{mol}/\text{m}^2/\text{s}$, plants become $\text{CO}_2$ limited. Supplementing the atmosphere to $1200-1500$ $\text{PPM}$ allows the plant to fully utilize the high light intensity, achieving maximum $\text{DLI}$ and corresponding biomass.

‚òÄÔ∏è Outdoor Cultivation: Terroir and Weather Defense

Outdoor growing is a partnership with nature. In Ohio, this means mastering soil biology, mitigating high humidity, and defending against the two biggest yield killers: **Botrytis (Bud Rot)** and **Corn Earworm**.

Chapter 1: Biologically Active Soil & Feeding

The Living Soil Ecosystem

True success comes from building a soil web rich in beneficial bacteria and fungi, creating a **Living Soil**. This allows for a $\text{Just-Add-Water}$ approach.

  • **Soil Amendments:** Use high-quality compost, worm castings, and slow-release organic inputs ($\text{Bone Meal}$, $\text{Kelp Meal}$, $\text{Oats}$) in the spring.
  • **Mycorrhizal Fungi:** Inoculate the root zone during transplanting with specific beneficial fungi (e.g., *Glomus intraradices*). They extend the root system's reach, dramatically improving water and nutrient uptake.
  • **Compost Tea:** Brewed $\text{Aerated Compost Tea}$ ($\text{ACT}$) is used as a soil drench or foliar spray to rapidly populate the soil and leaf surfaces with beneficial microbes, creating a natural barrier against pathogens.

Chapter 2: Mold Mitigation & Pest Defense

Late-Season Biosecurity in Ohio

  • **Preemptive BT Application:** Start applying *Bacillus thuringiensis* ($\text{BT}$) (specifically subspecies *kurstaki*) as a preventative spray starting in mid-August. $\text{BT}$ is a naturally occurring bacteria that kills caterpillars/budworms when ingested, but is safe for beneficials, humans, and pets. **Weekly application is essential.**
  • **Targeted Defoliation:** Aggressively remove large, non-essential fan leaves from the plant's interior starting in late $\text{August}$ to improve wind flow and reduce humidity pockets, drastically lowering the risk of $\text{Botrytis}$.
  • **Emergency Weather Protocol:** If heavy rain or frost is forecasted in September/October, be ready to cover plants with greenhouse plastic or harvest slightly early to preserve the crop.

üêõ Problem Hub: Clinical Diagnosis and IPM Tiers

Master the $\text{IPM}$ $(\text{Integrated Pest Management})$ Pyramid. We move from Prevention ($\text{Tier 1}$) to Biocontrol ($\text{Tier 3}$), reserving Chemical Control ($\text{Tier 4}$) for emergencies.

Tier 1 & 2: Cultural, Physical, and Prevention

  • **Quarantine:** New plants/clones must be quarantined for $\text{2-3}$ weeks before entering the main grow area.
  • **Scouting:** Use yellow and blue sticky traps at canopy level and soil level. A magnifying glass or jewelers loupe is required for inspecting the undersides of leaves.
  • **$\text{Fungus Gnat}$ Protocol:** The presence of small, dark flies at the soil level indicates a severe $\text{Fungus Gnat}$ infestation. Treat with $\text{BTi}$ soil drench (e.g., Mosquito Bits) and let the topsoil dry out completely between waterings.

Tier 4: Nutrient Pathologies (Mobile vs. Immobile)

Symptoms are classified by their mobility within the plant. Always check $\text{pH}$ first, as **lockout** is the primary cause.

  • **Mobile Nutrients ($\text{N, P, K, Mg}$):** Symptoms appear on **older, lower leaves** first (e.g., Nitrogen yellowing). The plant sacrifices old growth to feed new growth.
  • **Immobile Nutrients ($\text{Ca, Fe, Zn}$):** Symptoms appear on **newest, upper leaves** first (e.g., Calcium tip burn). The plant cannot move these nutrients once deposited.
  • **Toxicity (Burn):** Excess salt/nutrients ($\text{EC} > 3.0 \text{ mS}/\text{cm}$) causes brown, crispy tips (nute burn) and clawing leaves. **Immediate flush** with plain, $\text{pH}$-adjusted water is required.

üß¨ Genetics Lab: Breeding, Chemotypes, and Pheno-Hunting

Advanced cultivators select genetics based on desired chemical output ($\text{Chemotype}$), rather than arbitrary structure ($\text{Indica}/\text{Sativa}$).

Chapter 1: Cannabinoid and Terpene Genomics

The Chemotype System

The modern classification system categorizes cannabis by its dominant cannabinoids:

  • **Chemotype I:** $\text{THC}$-dominant. High psychoactivity. ($\text{THC}:\text{CBD}$ ratio $\text{> 10}:1$).
  • **Chemotype II:** Balanced $\text{THC}/\text{CBD}$. Moderate psychoactivity. ($\text{THC}:\text{CBD}$ ratio $\text{10}:1$ to $\text{1}:1$).
  • **Chemotype III:** $\text{CBD}$-dominant. Non-psychoactive. ($\text{THC}:\text{CBD}$ ratio $\text{< 1}:10$).
  • **Minor Cannabinoids:** $\text{CBG}$ (Cannabigerol) is the precursor to $\text{THC}$ and $\text{CBD}$. Advanced breeding focuses on "minor" chemotypes (e.g., $\text{THCV}$, $\text{CBC}$) for specific effects.

Terpenes modulate the effects of cannabinoids. Key terpenes to look for in breeder data:

Chapter 2: Pheno-Hunting and Breeding Basics

**Pheno-Hunting** is the process of growing multiple seeds from the same strain ($\text{F}1$ cross) to find the single plant ($\text{Phenotype}$) with the most desirable traits (vigor, yield, flavor, high $\text{THC}$ or $\text{CBD}$).

  • **$\text{F}1$ Hybrid:** The first generation cross between two distinct parent strains ($\text{P}1$). Exhibits **hybrid vigor** (rapid growth).
  • **$\text{F}2$ Generation:** Seeds produced from crossing two $\text{F}1$ siblings or backcrossing an $\text{F}1$ to a parent. This generation shows the most **genetic variation** and is where true pheno-hunting occurs.
  • **Cloning:** Once a desirable phenotype is found, it must be preserved via cloning (taking unrooted cuttings). This ensures $\text{100}\%$ genetic replication of the "Mother Plant."

‚öôÔ∏è Automation & IoT: The Data-Driven Grow Room

Move beyond manual control. Use microcontrollers and open-source software to build a self-regulating, data-logging $\text{CEA}$ environment, optimizing stability and maximizing resource efficiency.

Chapter 1: Open-Source Controller Systems

Hardware & Sensor Selection

  • **Controller:** Use **$\text{Raspberry Pi}$** ($\text{Linux}$ environment) or **$\text{ESP}32$** (microcontroller) for low-level sensor reading and relay control.
  • **Environmental Sensors:**
    • **Temp/RH:** $\text{BME}280$ or $\text{SHT}31$ (highly accurate, low drift). Required for calculating $\text{VPD}$.
    • **$\text{CO}_2$:** $\text{MH-Z}19$ or $\text{Senseair S}8$ ($\text{NDIR}$ sensors are required for accuracy).
    • **Nutrient:** $\text{EC}$ ($\text{mS}/\text{cm}$) and $\text{pH}$ probes (Industrial grade, require calibration and care).
  • **Actuators:** Solid State Relays ($\text{SSR}$) or power strips controlled by the microcontroller to switch lights, fans, and pumps ($\text{actuators}$) on and off.

Chapter 2: Data Logging and $\text{MQTT}$ Protocol

Data Structure (JSON) for Logging

All sensor readings should be logged in a standardized $\text{JSON}$ format for easy parsing and analysis. This enables $\text{API}$ integration and visualization (e.g., using $\text{Grafana}$ or $\text{Dash})$.

{ "timestamp": "2025-10-27T20:20:00Z", "device_id": "CEA-RPI-001", "environment": { "air_temp_c": 26.1, "rh_percent": 65.5, "vpd_kpa": 1.15, "co2_ppm": 412 }, "root_zone": { "water_temp_c": 20.5, "ph": 5.95, "ec_ms": 1.88, "rz_ec_runoff": 2.21 } }

Inter-Component Communication ($\text{MQTT}$)

The $\text{MQTT}$ protocol is the standard for $\text{IoT}$ communication. It is lightweight, efficient, and ensures reliable communication between the $\text{Pi}$, sensors, and a central server (broker).

  • **Broker:** A central server (often $\text{Mosquitto}$) where all data is sent.
  • **Publishers:** Sensors ($\text{temp}$, $\text{pH}$, $\text{EC}$) that publish data to a specific topic (e.g., $\text{growroom/env/temp}$).
  • **Subscribers:** Controllers or Dashboards that subscribe to topics to read data and trigger actions (e.g., if $\text{growroom/env/temp} > 28^{\circ}\text{C}$, activate the $\text{AC}$ relay).
